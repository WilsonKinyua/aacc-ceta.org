African Faiths Launch Network to Help Tackle Global Climate Change Crisis

African Faiths Launch Network to Help Tackle Global Climate Change Crisis

created on: Jun 27, 2022 by Comms Office 0

News

AACC | June 27, 2022

NAIROBI, Kenya

A climate change consultation in Kenya, stressed faith leaders’ role in accelerating action, as it explored how the groups could get involved in the on-going global discussions and processes on the global emergency.

At the June 20-24 meeting in the city of Nairobi -organized by the All Africa Conference of Churches (AACC) -the leaders heard that the churches’ voices against the crisis were frail.

But the leader at the consultation moved to boost the faiths' work, with the launch and commissioning of the Africa Faith Actors for Climate Justice (AFAN-CJ). The network has members from across Africa. It will work to amplify the organization’s work on climate change and accompany African leadership and its church membership in processes related to the emergency. It will also support the coordination of church work in the area so that it can inform the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change.

“We expect you to initiate and fulfil local, regional and international engagements because the only reason you have been chosen is that we know what you are already been doing. It is my hope however that you will be realistic and original. Not singing the same song as the whole world,” Mwombeki told the consultation at the commissioning of the network on June 23.

The leader said such realities would be – for example - campaigning against the use of charcoal among African communities when there is no affordable energy or banning bottled water when the right to safe drinking water for all has not been achieved.

“When we talk about plastics, it is not enough to say stop using plastics, it is also about how do we get clean water as a right,” said Mwombeki.

The meeting created a platform for faith leaders and their key technical stakeholders to deliberate on critical issues related to climate change. It also engaged in eco-theological debates and highlighted best practices, such as climate-resilient agriculture in the experience of Zambia’s Mindolo Ecumenical Foundation and a climate-resilient church in Burundi.

According to the conference document, environmental issues confronting the world today require immediate attention and effective action by all stakeholders. The rapidly changing environmental systems and depleting resources are causing substantial concern to leaders around the world. Yet, the church should also be a major actor in finding sustainable solutions.

“Churches have not taken their responsibility on climate change seriously. The issue is a matter of life and death,” Rev. Dr. Ezekiel Lesmore, the director of programmes at the AACC told the meeting’s opening. “We are here discussing an issue that has to do with life and death. Let this offer a challenge to each one of us. We would like to confess that we have abdicated our role in ecological care.”

African countries are bearing the brunt of climate change, among other environmental challenges, despite contributing the least to earth’s destruction, notes AACC. Communities in the continent are facing droughts, floods and reduced crop yields and livestock capacities, and climate sensitive diseases such as malaria and cholera.

At the grassroots, according to the Rev. Dr. Joseph Mutie, a lecturer at the St. Pauls University in Limuru, Kenya, the churches were leading in raising the alarm about climate change challenges such as droughts. Religious organizations has also started environmental programmes, according to the lecturer, but the groups are not at the key decision making table.

“When they are done, we can only react,” said Mutie. “We are in this together and if we don’t stay together, we will sink. This is like a ship if it sinks, we all sink together.”

At the same time, Arthur Becker, Liberia’s Representative at the 3rd Permanent General assembly – African Union and an Environment and Climate Change expert told the consultation that Faith Based Organizations have been recognised as important players in protecting the environment and working towards climate justice.

“Research shows that religion and faith can play a vital role in legitimising societal values. World faiths represent global identities and the shared values that influence the way people live and act especially in harmony with nature,” said Becker. “[They can] help galvanise local communities to coordinate and adopt solutions that mitigate climate change’s effect.”
